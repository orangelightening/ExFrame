Query: What is a psychotic episode

A psychotic episode refers to a period during which a person loses some contact with reality, experiencing symptoms that disrupt their thoughts, perceptions, and behaviors. This condition is characterized by an impaired ability to distinguish between what is real and what is not.

The main symptoms of a psychotic episode include:

- **Hallucinations**: Sensory experiences that occur without an external stimulus, such as hearing voices, seeing things, or feeling sensations that are not present. Hearing voices is a common example.
- **Delusions**: Strongly held false beliefs that are not aligned with cultural norms and cannot be corrected by logical reasoning. Examples include beliefs of being persecuted, having special powers, or receiving secret messages from media.
- **Disorganized thinking and speech**: Thoughts may become jumbled or racing, leading to speech that is difficult to follow, with frequent topic shifts or incoherent content.

Additional symptoms may include inappropriate emotional responses, paranoia, confusion, and difficulties with concentration, memory, and daily functioning. Some individuals may also experience mood disturbances, suicidal thoughts, or substance use issues during or around the episode.

A psychotic episode typically unfolds in three phases:

1. **Prodromal phase**: A gradual onset with subtle changes such as social withdrawal, reduced motivation, sleep disturbances, irritability, and difficulties with concentration. These signs may precede the full episode by weeks or months.
2. **Acute phase**: The active stage where clear psychotic symptoms like hallucinations and delusions are present. This phase is usually the most recognizable and often prompts treatment.
3. **Recovery (or residual) phase**: Symptoms lessen in intensity, though some may persist. Recovery varies by individual, and ongoing treatment can help prevent relapse.

Psychosis can occur as part of various mental health conditions such as schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, or severe depression, or it can be triggered by factors like trauma, stress, substance use, or medical conditions. It may also appear for the first time in late adolescence or early adulthood, though it can happen at any age.

Approximately 3% of people will experience a psychotic episode at some point in their lives. Episodes can last from a few hours to several months. Early intervention significantly improves outcomes, and treatment often includes antipsychotic medication, psychotherapy (such as cognitive behavioral therapy), and social support.

In some cases, a first episode of psychosis‚Äîcalled first episode psychosis (FEP)‚Äîmay be the only one a person experiences, with about 25% never having another episode. For others, ongoing management is necessary to maintain recovery and prevent future episodes.

Neurosis is an outmoded term historically used in psychology and psychiatry to describe a range of mental disorders characterized by persistent anxiety, depression, obsessive thoughts, compulsive behaviors, or other forms of emotional distress. Individuals with neuroses typically maintain a clear connection to reality, distinguishing neurosis from psychosis, where there is a loss of contact with reality, such as through hallucinations or delusions.

The term was first introduced in 1769 by Scottish physician William Cullen, who used it to describe nervous system disorders without a clear physiological cause. Over time, especially through the work of Sigmund Freud and Carl Jung, neurosis came to be understood as a psychological condition arising from unresolved internal conflicts, repressed emotions, or past trauma. Freud viewed neurosis as a maladaptive coping mechanism, while Jung saw it as a clash between conscious and unconscious elements of the mind.

In modern clinical practice, the term "neurosis" is no longer used in official diagnostic systems such as the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5) or the International Classification of Diseases (ICD-11). Although the term is outdated in clinical contexts, it may still appear in popular discourse or psychoanalytic theory.
